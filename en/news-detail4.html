Common valuation mistakes commented by court

We summarize some common valuation mistakes occurred in valuation as a support to court litigations:
  1. Lacking details and explanations about how to arrive at that conclusion
    Insufficient explanations so that other professionals could not replicate work
  2. Pure Reliance on Case Law for Discount adjustment
    Although discount adjustment is essentially case-by-case factual issue, pure reliance on case law for determination of valuation discounts is inadvisable
  3. Lack of Independence
    Acting as valuators and appraisers
  4. Failure to Find Available Information
    Very few things look worse for a valuator than when he or she cannot find information that the opposing valuator finds.
  5. Insufficient Explanation of Assumptions
    An expert report that is based on estimates and assumptions not supported by independent evidence or verification is of little probative value or assistance to the court.
  6. Making the Hypothetical Buyer Too Real
    While the fair value measures assumes hypothetical buyer and seller. Court judges the hypothetical sale should not be constructed in a vacuum isolated from the actual facts that affect value.
  7. Failure to Think Like An Investor
    Have not considered as a willing buyer to use the subtraction method in deciding the value of the stock.
  8. 1. Undue Reliance of the Work of Other Valuators
    The relying valuator cannot blindly rely on the work of others, but must make some baseline assessment of the accuracy and completeness of the other appraiser’s work.
  9. Exceeding margin of error
    Valuation can be negligent if it is outside a "permissible margin for error", usually set at 15%.
