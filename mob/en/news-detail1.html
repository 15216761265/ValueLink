US SEC views on cheap stock issue

One of usual topics appeared on US SEC comment letters during US IPO process is the cheap stock issue. Of the 191 companies that undertook an IPO in 2018, 125, or approximately 65%, received a SEC staff comment letter that contained at least one cheap stock comment. SEC is concerned about the reliability of stock value estimates. SEC typically scrutinizes valuation of stock-based activity in the period 12-18 months prior to the IPO. The authority requires issuers to disclose the value of the common stock in each ESOP issuance rounds. If the issuers the issued many rounds of ESOP or performed several rounds of equity financing before IPO, the disclosed value of common shares will form a trend across time. Consequently, SEC will require explanation on factors leading to the value trend of common stock. SEC will enquire issuers to compare the value of common stock used in ESOP estimate with issuer's other stock valuations for purposes such as impairment test, equity financing. They may also reconcile stock valuation with pricing discussion with underwriters or reconcile the stock valuation to estimated IPO price range.
SEC’s practice of assessing and issuing cheap stock comments late in the registration process (generally, just as the company is beginning the investor road show) when it obtained the estimated IPO price range for judgement. SEC’s practice of assessing and issuing cheap stock comments late in the registration process (generally, just as the company is beginning the investor road show) when it obtained the estimated IPO price range for judgement. So, one of major risks of cheap stock issue is delaying the IPO process when this issue was raised at late stage. To mitigate the risk, issuer can:
  • Issuer should appoint a valuer to conduct a contemporaneous independent valuation. In issuer's disclosure, issuer should highlight the independence of the valuation consultant.
  • Issuer should also discuss their valuation approach in the Management’s Discussion and Analysis section of their IPO registration statement.
  • Issuer should pro-actively keep in touch with SEC and take a preemptive approach to SEC review by submitting a stand-alone “cheap stock letter”.
